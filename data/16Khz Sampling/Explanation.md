This experiment was extended from the earlier 8 kHz system to investigate whether increasing the sampling rate to 16 kHz would improve frequency detection accuracy and overall signal quality in an STM32-based audio acquisition system. While the 8 kHz setup had already shown stable and usable results for frequencies below approximately 4 kHz, the goal of the 16 kHz version was to capture a wider frequency range and improve resolution for more complex signals such as bird calls.

The first step in this transition was a simple change in the system configuration, where only the audio frequency in CubeMX was increased from 8 kHz to 16 kHz while keeping the rest of the system unchanged. This immediately led to unexpected behavior in the recorded signals. Instead of cleaner results, the frequency detection became unstable and produced shifted or inconsistent peaks in the FFT analysis. Even though the system was theoretically operating at a higher sampling rate, the measured frequencies did not align correctly with the expected values.

To address this, the data acquisition method was modified. Instead of relying on a fixed 5-second time-based recording approach, the system was changed to a byte-based recording method. In this approach, the amount of data collected was controlled by the expected number of bytes (based on sampling rate x duration x  sample size) rather than relying on time. This adjustment was intended to ensure that each recording contained a consistent amount of data regardless of small timing or streaming delays in the system. While this improved consistency in data collection, the frequency detection still showed noticeable errors.

After this, attention was shifted to the clock configuration inside the STM32 system. The PLLSAI configuration was modified in an attempt to reduce the sampling error and bring the real audio frequency closer to the intended 16 kHz. During this process, different divider combinations were tested, including configurations such as using /17 in the PLLSAI path. However, this configuration was not optimal because it introduced non-ideal clock division ratios, which increased instability in the generated audio clock. Instead of improving accuracy, it led to less stable timing behavior, which negatively affected FFT results and frequency detection reliability.

Through further testing and comparison, it became clear that the system had a fundamental limitation in how precisely it could generate a clean 16 kHz sampling rate using the available PLLSAI configuration options. Even after switching to HSE and optimizing PLL parameters, the best achievable configuration resulted in a real sampling rate with approximately 11.6% error when using the most stable divider setup (such as x8 with /7). Although this is not an exact match to 16 kHz, it provided the most stable and consistent signal behavior across all tests.

The final conclusion of this stage of the experiment was that the STM32 system, under the given clock constraints and PLLSAI limitations, cannot achieve a perfectly accurate 16 kHz sampling rate. However, the configuration using the x8 PLL multiplier with /7 division was determined to be the most reliable setup, offering the best balance between stability and frequency accuracy. This setup, although not ideal in terms of absolute sampling precision, provided the most consistent results for FFT-based frequency detection and was therefore considered the best practical solution for the system.
